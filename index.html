Discussion Board

Why is encryption important?

Encryption protects data from theft, alteration, or compromise by converting it into a secret code accessible only with a unique digital key.

Data encryption is important because it helps protect people's privacy, and secures data from attackers and other cybersecurity threats. Learn more.

Can encryption be misused?

Attackers can use encryption to hold a victim's data or device hostage, demanding payment in exchange for the decryption key. This type of attack, known as ransomware, is becoming increasingly common and can cause significant financial or reputational damage to victims.

Misuse of cryptography can include using encryption to hide the contents of illegal messages, using stolen or fake digital certificates to impersonate legitimate entities, or using weak or compromised encryption algorithms that can be easily broken. Read more.

Scenarios

Encrypted ransomware: Holding digital assets hostage

In the world of cyber threats, ransomware has become increasingly infamous. These malicious software strains encrypt victims' files or entire systems, holding them ransom until a fee, often demanded in cryptocurrency, is paid. Due to the strong encryption algorithms employed, victims are typically unable to regain access to their files without the decryption key held by the attacker. This form of cyber-extortion has targeted not only individuals but major corporations, hospitals, and even city governments. Read More.

Terrorism & Extremist Groups

Scenario: Terrorist organizations use encrypted chat apps and emails to recruit members, plan attacks, and exchange confidential data. Authorities find it difficult to track threats in real time because encryption hides conversations from surveillance.
Real Case: ISIS and other extremist groups have used Telegram and Signal to spread propaganda and organize activities while evading intelligence agencies. Read More.

Discussion on real-life ethical concerns about encryption

The ethical dilemma surrounding encryption revolves around the tension between privacy rights and national security imperatives. On one hand, advocating for strong encryption is paramount to safeguarding individual privacy, ensuring freedom of speech, and fostering trust in digital technologies.

Explore ethical concerns.

Privacy vs. National Security

One of the most contentious issues surrounding encryption is the balance between personal privacy and national security. Encryption allows individuals to communicate securely, protecting them from cyber threats and surveillance. However, governments argue that encryption also enables criminals and terrorists to evade law enforcement.
A well-known case highlighting this debate is the Apple vs. FBI dispute in 2016. The FBI requested Apple to unlock an iPhone belonging to a terrorist, but Apple refused, stating that creating a backdoor would weaken security for all users. Encryption Backfoors: A Double-Edged Sword

Governments and law enforcement agencies worldwide demand backdoors in encryption to access data during criminal investigations. However, security experts warn that introducing backdoors compromises encryption for everyone, making systems vulnerable to cyberattacks.
For instance, the UK government has repeatedly called for companies like WhatsApp to weaken encryption for national security reasons.
